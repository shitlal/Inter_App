 Chairman's Message      

Credit guarantee schemes funded by the Government with liberal budgetary support exist in various countries in one form or the other. In India, credit guarantee scheme was first introduced by Reserve Bank of India (RBI) in late Sixties with a view to encouraging the lenders to enhance availability of credit for small borrowers. Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation, a subsidiary of RBI, has been extending guarantee support in respect of credit to small scale industrial (SSI) units since 1981. However, over time, the guarantee scheme did not find acceptance by the lenders. Nevertheless, credit flow by the banking system to SSIs was not impaired. While bank credit to SSIs in absolute terms had increased in the last decade, the first generation entrepreneurs especially technocrats have been finding it difficult to access bank credit, without offering any collateral security. It is also true that banks perceive lending to small borrowers as high risk and insist on collateral security to increase their comfort level. Policy makers, therefore, felt the need to introduce a new scheme in consonance with the emerging requirements particularly in resolving the problem of collateral security and enhancing credit to the targeted groups.

    Hon'ble Union Finance Minister, in his budget speech for 1999-2000, had indicated, "Inability to provide adequate security to banks and low recovery are often cited as a major constraint in flow of investment credit to SSI units. The problem is more acute for export oriented and tiny sector enterprises. To alleviate this problem, a new credit insurance scheme will be launched." As a follow up measure, Ministry of SSI and ARI in consultation with Small Industries Development Bank of India (SIDBI) formulated a new credit guarantee scheme viz. Credit Guarantee Fund (Scheme) for Small Industries (CGFSI), covering credit facility to SSIs up to Rs.10 lakh extended without any collateral or third party guarantee by the scheduled commercial banks and select Regional Rural Banks.

    For the purpose, an outfit known as Credit Guarantee Fund Trust for Small Industries has been set up, registered at Mumbai on July 27, 2000. The Board of Trustees comprise CMD, SIDBI, who is ex-officio Chairman; Additional Secretary & Development Commissioner, Ministry of SSI & ARI, who is ex-officio Vice-Chairman; Executive Director (Priority Sector), RBI and the Chief Executive Officer, who is the Member Secretary. The day-to-day affairs are looked after by Shri N. Venkatasubramanyan, CEO.

    A corpus fund of Rs.2,500 crore,will be built over a period of five years, with contributions from Government of India and SIDBI in ratio 4:1. The Trust would endeavour to provide guarantee cover in respect of loans to targeted sectors in SSI of the order of Rs.21,000 crore over the period of five years i.e. from 2000-01 to 2004-05. In addition to enhancing the flow of collateral free credit to small borrowers, the main benefits envisaged from the Scheme include (a) additional output of the order of Rs.70,000 crore, (b) 5 per cent increase in capacity utilisation of existing SSI units, (c) additional employment generation of 16 lakh persons, (d) increase in export earnings by Rs.7,000 crore, and (e) additional excise revenue to the tune of Rs.5,300 crore.

    The new scheme has come in to effect from August 1, 2000. Details of the scheme are put in the web-page. The entire operations would be based on B2B e-business concept so as to offer real time, efficient service to the customers.

    I urge upon Captains of the commercial banks to become eligible institutions and avail of the guarantee facility in respect of their collateral free loans to SSIs.

Thank you.

P.B. Nimbalkar
